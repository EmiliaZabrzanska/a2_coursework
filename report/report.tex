\noindent Figures 3 and 4 show the FBP and GD reconstructions.

\noindent \textbf{When do they do better or worse?}

\noindent FBP performs better when the dose is high and there is sufficient angular converage. 
It is also much faster and produces an image almost instantaneously. 
The default filter used in FBP is Ram-Lak, which preserves high frequency detail, resulting in images with sharp edges, critical for clinical cases.
However, this algorithm degrades rapidly at lower doses due to the equal amplification of high frequencies, regardless of whether they represent noise or signal.\\

\noindent GD performs better when dose is reduced. It does not amplify noise in the same way as FBP, but fits sinogram data progressively resulting in better oerformance at that level. 
It also does better when angular coverage is sparse, handling undersampled data much better than FBP.
The reconstructions from GD are softer than FBP as the images are built up from zero initialisations with no sharpening filter. 
FBP therefore outperforms here, as edge sharpness is extremely important in clinical CT. \\

\noindent A key point to consider is while FBP produces images instantly, GD takes time to perform iterations, increasing the more we do. 
This poses a significant difference in clinical settings, but also creates an important dilemma for GD, as we need to be able to balance the number of iterations with the time taken to reconstruct them.

\noindent \textbf{Clinical implication}

\noindent Reducing the source intensity ($I_0$) reduces the radiation dose, which is important as X-rays are a form of ionising radiation. 
Limiting dose has a meaningful impact on patient safety, especially those with chronic or long term conditions receiving many scans. 
The trade off here is that as the dose is lowered, it becomes very difficult to reconstruct. \\

\noindent Reducing the number of projections shortens the total scan time, and can reduce patient discomfort or anxiety, but also motion artefacts, as patients will not move or breathe as much during a shorter scan. 
This is valuable for constantly moving organs such as the heart or lungs, but also for cases, where patients find it harder to remain still. \\

\noindent FBP produces instant results, along with sharp and high quality images at standard doses with enough angles.
This is vital in emergency situations where clinicians need scans to be ready straight away. 
The disadvantage of FBP is that results are extremely sensitive to dose reduction, so are unstable for low dose CT. \\

\noindent Gradient Descent can withstand noise and sparse data better than FBP, but comes at a significant computational cost. 
In clinical practice this is addressed through the use of GPU accelerated implementations, but these still do not acheive that instant reconstructions that FBP does. 
Another issue with GD is the softer appearance of edges. This is a disadvantage in clincial imaging, as sharp edges are crucial for detecting anatomy and its margins, as well as performing measurements. \\

\noindent \textbf{Impact}

\noindent Reducing $I_0$ from $10^5$ to $10^3$ represents a $100\times$ reduction in radiation dose, which has an enourmous impact for patients undergoing regular scans as part of their treatment. 
Being subjected to a lower dose each time significantly reduces the radiation the patients are exposed to, and therefore the risk of cancer or other exposure related complications. 
However, as demonstrated by the FBP results, reducing dose significantly impacts image reconstruction, meaning that while low dose CT is exceptionally meaningful, it is only a viable option when paired with iterative or deep learning reconstructions. \\

\noindent Reducing angle projections from 360 to 20 cuts the scan time by approximately $18\times$. 
This kind of reduction is very important where scans must be completed as quickly as possible before they are destroyed by motion. 
This is also meaningful for emergency imaging, where faster imaging results in faster diagnosis and treatment.  \\

\noindent The choice of algorithm also has a clear impact on the outcome. 
FBP's near instant results are critical in time sensitive settings like emergency medicine, but suffer when dose is reduced, whereas GD produces better quality images at reduced doses, but requires significantly more computation and time, which is ideal when low dose is necessary, but the results aren't needed urgently.\\

\subsubsection*{(d) Methods for improvement}

\noindent Adding a regulariser to the Gradient Descent minimisation problem helps encode prior knowledge about what the final image should look like. 
One example is Total Variation, which turns the problem into the following:

$$\hat{\mu} = \arg\min_\mu \|A\mu - p\|^2 + \alpha R(\mu)$$

\noindent where the regularisation term is:

$$TV(\mu) = \|\nabla\mu\|_1 = \sum_{i,j} \sqrt{|\mu_{i,j+1} - \mu_{i,j}| + |\mu_{i+1,j} - \mu_{i,j}|}$$

\noindent TV penalises the sum of image gradients, promoting piecewise constant solutions with sharp edges, but still maintaining anatomical boundaries. 
Aside from TV, we can also use L1 or L2 norms as regularisers in reconstruction problems. \\

\noindent We can also implement Deep Learning denoising, which involves training a model such as a UNet, GAN or Diffusiion model to learn an improved image from a worse one. 
While this is very computationally efficient and versatile, the method relies heavily on its trainig data, and deep learning models can hallucinate, removing or adding artefacts to the image. \\

\noindent Non-Local means can be applied as a regulariser, and will search the entire image to find similar patches and average them together, before replacing this into the original patches. 
It is very useful for reducing noise while preserving texture and and structural details, so is better than local filtering methods.\\

\subsubsection*{(c) Mathematical principles}

The mathematical principal describing this is the Fourier Slice Theorem, which stated that a 1D fourier transform of the detector function (a projection) at an angle $\theta$ is the same as a line or radial slice through the origin of the 2D fourier transform of the image at the same angle. 
The more projections that we take, the more radial slices we obtain.
At a limited range however, only the radial lines corresponding to the 40 degree wedge are populated with radial lines. 
No reconstructive algorithm can recover information that is not in the data, which results in deficiencies, and regardless of how many projections we take in this range, there will be almost no improvement due to the data needed simply not being there. \\

\noindent As discussed in Lecture 11 (02/03/2026), edges appear when there is a sharp change in the brightness or intensity in the image. 
We characterise them using the image gradient, where the gradient vector points in the direction of maximum intensity change, and its magnitude is largest at an edge. 
Therefore, the direction of an edge is perpendicular to the direction of the gradient vector. 
Connected with the Fourier Slice Theorem, an edge will only be visible in CT if the projection angle perpendicular to it lies within the measured range. \\

\end{figure}

\noindent Both methods produce near-identical final reconstructions (RMSE 0.00021, PSNR $\approx$13.4~dB, SSIM $\approx$0.20), as expected since they solve the same minimisation problem. The convergence plot shows OS-SART's advantage: it reaches a low residual much faster because it completes the equivalent of 10 SIRT iterations in one pass through the data. SIRT takes approximately 20 iterations to reach the residual at which OS-SART begins. After this initial advantage, both curves converge to the same final residual. OS-SART's subset updates are individually noisier, but this averages out over time.

\subsubsection*{(d) MLEM/OSEM vs SIRT}

\noindent Both CT and PET reconstructions can be framed as minimisation problems, however the type of noise encountered in each type of imaging is different, meaning that the algoriths used for reconstructions will also differ. \\

\noindent The forward model in CT is $p = -\log(\frac{I}{I_0}) = \sum_i \mu_i l_i$, where the dominant noise is a mix of predominantly electronic Gaussian noise and Poisson photon counting noise. 
The maximum likelihood estimate for Gaussian noise is obtained using least squares, which is the minimisation used in Gradient Descent (SIRT) algorithms.\\

\noindent PET detects individual photon pairs from positron annihilation events. 
This is a discrete counting process where the acquisition model is $I_j^{em} = \sum_i I_i l_{ij}$, and $I_j^{em}$ is the number of detected photon pairs along the line of response $j$. 
Because PET is counting individual events, its noise follows a Poisson distribution.
Due to the nature of the noise, we cannot use a least squares minimisation, but maximise using the Poisson log likelihood.\\

\noindent MLEM uses a multiplicative update rather than the additive step used in SIRT, which naturally controls non-negativity and correctly accounts for the Poisson statistics of the measurements, whereas OSEM is the ordered subset version of MLEM, the PET equivalent of using OS-SART in CT. 
It splits data into subsets to accelerate the convergence of the MLEM algorithm, providing a good reconstruction faster than MLEM.\\

\noindent In summary, we use MLEM and OSEM in PET because the reconstruction algorithm must match the noise model of the data. 
While CT is mainly Gaussian and therefore suits SIRT like algorithms, PET measurements are dominated by Poisson noise, requiring a statistically correct algorithm that reflects this.

Another method to implement would be combining the coils by weighting them according to their individual sensitivities rather than through equal weight root sum of squares.

\subsection*{Exercise 3.1: Critical Comparison of Methods}

\subsubsection*{(a) Selected publications}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Non-ML/DL:} Khorshidi, ‘Tumor Segmentation via Enhanced Area Growth Algorithm for Lung CT Images’. (2023)
    \item \textbf{DL:} Kashyap et al., ‘Automated Deep Learning–Based Detection and Segmentation of Lung Tumors at CT Imaging’. (2025)
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{(b) Summaries and critical comparison}

\subsubsection{Paper summaries:}

\noindent The Khorshidi, (2023) paper presents an Enhanced Area Growth algorithm (EAG) to segment lung CT images. 
It aims to enhance a classical region growing approach, where a user selects a point inside the tumour, and the algorithm expands outwards by checking the colour intensity values of neighbouring pixels. 
The basic algorithm performance was reliant on the user selection, and struggled when the tumor was attached to lung walls. 
It was enhanced using contrast augmentation to preprocess images, local thresholding, growing from multiple points surrounding the user's seed, and adding in an edge refinement step. 
The EAG algorithmwas tested on 60 CT scans across DIR, LCA, NSCLC and LIDC datasets, and the Dice coefficient was $0.92 \pm 0.03$, compared to $0.80 \pm 0.02$ for the basic growth algorithm. 
Table 10 in the paper gives the accuracy of the algorithm as $92.1\%$ for the EAG, compared to $84.7\%$ for the basic algorithm.\\

\noindent The Kashyap et al., (2025) paper develops a 3D U-Net ensemble model for detecting and segmenting lung tumors on CT scans. 
It was trained on 1504 pre-radiotherapy CT simulation scans and the associated clinical 3D segmentations used for radiation therapy planning from 1295 patients. 
The ensemble combines 2 3D U-Nets, one trained for high resolution and small input volume, and one trained for low resolution and large input volume, acheiving a 92\% sensitivity and 82\% specificity in tumor detection, and a median Dice coefficient of 0.77 for segmentation. 
The model was also successful in reducing the time taken to segment tumors, taking an average of 76.6 seconds compared to over 160 seconds for manual contouring. 
The model was also evaluated on a separate dataset containing a mean on 4.95 tumors per scan, and acheived a sensitivity of 67\%.

\newpage

\subsubsection{Critical comparison:}

\noindent The papers both aim to tackle the same clinical problem of accurate lung tumour segmentation on CT images. \\

\noindent The EAG algorithm is semi-automatic, requiring a user to select the start point and specify the maximum tumour radius prior to the algorithm running. 
This requires the user to have good anatomical knowledge and be able to correctly read and identify the different areas on a CT. 
While the Enhanced Area Growth algorithm implements the multipoint-growth-starting-point, the quality of the results is still dependent on the original seed selected. 
In contrast the deep learning model is entirely automatic, taking raw CT inputs and outputting both the tumour detection and segmentation without any guidance. 
This is major advantage, especially for workflows where manual interaction for every scan would not be feasible or where users lack expertise.\\

\noindent The EAG algorithm reported a higher Dice coefficient of 0.92, compared to 0.77 in the deep learning model. 
However, the EAG algorithm was only evaluated on 60 images, and the ground truth was only delineated by a single expert, compared to the deep learning model's evaluation on 100 images compared to three physician segmentations per scan, comprising of one clinical and two research segmentations. 
It is also important to note that the EAG algorithm uses a seed point that already tells it where the tumour is, while the deep learning model has to detect it first. 
Furthermore, the deep learning model also provided an interphysician Dice coefficient of 0.80, which when compared to the model's value of 0.77 suggests that the model is segmenting tumors at a near expert level. 
This is another big strength of Kashyap et al., as we are provided with a clear benchmark that allows direct comparison between human and model performence. \\

\noindent Both methods illustrate different challenges, with the EAG algorithm being unable to segment fragmented or multiple tumours, and struggling when they impede onto the lung wall or are located in areas of similar colour intensity. 
The paper discusses a lung masking step to help mitigate this issue, but it doesn't fully resolve it, as the algorithm cannot distinguish the tumour when surrounded by similar tissue. 
The deep learning model struggled with very large tumours, particularly those invading the mediastinum or chest wall.
The EAG algrithm performed much better on this, as it could freely interpolate between its multiple growth points over the entire tumour. \\

\noindent In terms of accesibility, the EAG model is smaller and works immediately on a single image with minimal prior information, so can be used by virtually anyone due to its minimal compute requirements and setup time. 
The deep learning model requires training on a large dataset, and requires GPU infrastructure for both training and inference, which is not readily available. 
Despite this, once set up, the model is able to segment tumors in roughly 77 seconds compared to an avergae of 160 seconds for physicians, which is a significant improvement.\\

\noindent In summary, the Enhanced Area Growth algorithm is much simpler to implement and requires minimal computational resources while still being able to acheive very high accuracy scores on well defined tumours. 
However, the algorithm is semi-automatic and very parameter sensitive, so will only acheive this level of performance in ideal scenarios, and has not been validated very rigorously, using only a single expert reference, which opens questions about the reliability of the benchmarks. 
The deep learning approach is evaluated much more thoroughly, reporting detection and segmentation metrics, volume agreement analysis and timing comparisons. 
The evaluation also implements a negative control set and benchmarks against three independent physicians. 
The model does however require much more data and compute to develop, but once trained it is fully automatic and approaches expert level performance, making it better suited to large scale tasks. \\

\subsubsection*{(c) Proposed improvements}

\noindent Focusing on the deep learning paper, there are several key areas for improvement to explore. 
The 3D patch-based approach of the paper results in difficulties detecting and segmenting large tumors as they may not fit within a single patch. 
Implementing a coarse to fine pipeline could aid in solving this problem, as by using a first pass model that operates on the entire CT volume or with very large patches, tumours can be roughly identified before refining the segmentation with a second pass model that focuses on the areas around each detected tumour. 
This way, the first pass would be able to capture large tumours and improve the model's overall performance in this area.\\

\noindent Another improvement worth exploring is expanding the dataset the model is trained on. 
At present, the model is trained on CT images where not all of the tumours are segmented. 
The dataset includes a mix of images containing only one tumour, or multiple tumours where not all of them are always labelled. 
Ensuring all tumours are accurately labelled and segmented, as well as including more images contianing multiple tumours would help generalise the model, and improve its performance in multi tumour evaluation, which currently sits at 67\%.\\

\noindent Incorporating PET data is also an important advancement, as the worst reported cases all involved tumours that invaded into the chest wall or mediastinum, and CT alone makes these boundaries too ambiguous to distinguish. 
PET data highlights tumours from the surrounding tissue, and could be incorporated into the model as an additional input dimension, which would not only make the model perform better, but also more suited to a variety of lung tumours, straying away from the need for perfect data and towards success in real clinical settings. 
This would require more training on a mix of CT and PET datasets, but would overall be a big improvement.
