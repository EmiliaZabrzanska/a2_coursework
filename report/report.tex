\noindent \textbf{When do they do better or worse?}

\noindent FBP performs better when the dose is high and there is sufficient angular converage. 
It is also much faster and produces an image almost instantaneously. 
The default filter used in FBP is Ram-Lak, which preserves high frequency detail, resulting in images with sharp edges, critical for clinical cases.
However, this algorithm degrades rapidly at lower doses due to the equal amplification of high frequencies, regardless of whether they represent noise or signal.\\

\noindent GD performs better when dose is reduced. It does not amplify noise in the same way as FBP, but fits sinogram data progressively resulting in better oerformance at that level. 
It also does better when angular coverage is sparse, handling undersampled data much better than FBP.
The reconstructions from GD are softer than FBP as the images are built up from zero initialisations with no sharpening filter. 
FBP therefore outperforms here, as edge sharpness is extremely important in clinical CT. \\

\noindent A key point to consider is while FBP produces images instantly, GD takes time to perform iterations, increasing the more we do. 
This poses a significant difference in clinical settings, but also creates an important dilemma for GD, as we need to be able to balance the number of iterations with the time taken to reconstruct them.

\noindent \textbf{Clinical implication}

\noindent Reducing the source intensity ($I_0$) directly reduces the radiation dose delivered to the patient, which is important as X-rays are a form of ionising radiation, and can damage DNA with enough exposure. 
Limiting dose has a meaningful impact on patient safety, especially those with chronic or long term conditions receiving many scans. 
The trade off here is that as the dose is lowered, it becomes very difficult to reconstruct. \\

\noindent Reducing the number of projections shortens the total scan time, which not only reduces patient discomfort or anxiety, but also motion artefacts, as patients will not move or breathe as much during a shorter scan. 
This is valuable for constantly moving organs such as the heart or lungs, but also for paediatric cases, where children find it harder to remain stil. 
Despite these advantages, sparse view CT introduces streak artefacts, which could be misinterpreted as lesions.\\

\noindent FBP produces instant results, along with sharp and high quality images at standard doses with enough angles. 
In clinical practice this means that reconstructions are available straight away, and show all boundaries and edges clearly. 
This is vital in emergency situations where clinicians need scans to be ready straight away. 
The disadvantage of FBP is that results are extremely sensitive to dose reduction, so are unstable for low dose CT. \\

\noindent Gradient Descent can withstand noise and sparse data better than FBP, but comes at a significant computational cost. 
In clinical practice this is addressed through the use of GPU accelerated implementations, but these still do not acheive that instant reconstructions that FBP does. 
Another issue with GD is the softer appearance of edges. This is a disadvantage in clincial imaging, as sharp edges are crucial for detecting anatomy and its margins, as well as performing measurements. \\

\noindent \textbf{Impact}

\noindent Reducing $I_0$ from $10^5$ to $10^3$ represents a $100\times$ reduction in radiation dose, which has an enourmous impact for patients undergoing regular scans as part of their treatment. 
Being subjected to a lower dose each time significantly reduces the radiation the patients are exposed to, and therefore the risk of cancer or other exposure related complications. 
However, as demonstrated by the FBP results, reducing dose significantly impacts image reconstruction, meaning that while low dose CT is exceptionally meaningful, it is only a viable option when paired with iterative or deep learning reconstructions. \\

\noindent Reducing angle projections from 360 to 20 cuts the scan time by approximately $18\times$. 
This kind of reduction is very important where scans must be completed as quickly as possible before they are destroyed by motion. 
This is also meaningful for emergency imaging, where faster imaging results in faster diagnosis and treatment.  \\

\noindent The choice of algorithm also has a clear impact on the outcome. 
FBP's near instant results are critical in time sensitive settings like emergency medicine, but suffer when dose is reduced, whereas GD produces better quality images at reduced doses, but requires significantly more computation and time, which is ideal when low dose is necessary, but the results aren't needed urgently.\\

\subsubsection*{(d) Methods for improvement}

\noindent Adding a regulariser to the Gradient Descent minimisation problem helps encode prior knowledge about what the final image should look like. One example is Total Variation, which turns the problem into the following:

$$\hat{\mu} = \arg\min_\mu \|A\mu - p\|^2 + \alpha R(\mu)$$

\noindent where the regularisation term is:

$$TV(\mu) = \|\nabla\mu\|_1 = \sum_{i,j} \sqrt{|\mu_{i,j+1} - \mu_{i,j}| + |\mu_{i+1,j} - \mu_{i,j}|}$$

\noindent TV penalises the sum of image gradients, which promotes piecewise constant solutions with sharp edges, directly addressing the noise seen in our reconstructions, but still maintaining anatomical boundaries. Aside from TV, we can also use L1 or L2 norms as regularisers in reconstruction problems. \\

\noindent We can also implement Deep Learning denoising, which involves training a model such as a UNet, GAN, Diffusiion model etc. to learn an improved image from a worse one. This allows reconstructions to still use FBP and acheive instant results when needed, but then be passed through a model to reduce noise and improve quality. While this is very computationally efficient and versatile, the method relies heavily on its trainig data, and can be subject to hallucinations, where the model may remove or add artefacts from the image and affect the patient's diagnosis. \\

\noindent Non-Local means is another denoising method, which can be applied as a regulariser but also in post-processing. NLM will search the entire image to find patches with a similar structure and average them together, before replacing this average into the original patches. It is very useful for reducing noise while preserving texture and and structural details, so is better than local filtering methods. The trade off is the computational cost, as it is significantly more demanding than simpler methods.\\

\subsubsection*{(c) Mathematical principles}

The mathematical principal describing this is the Fourier Slice Theorem, which stated that a 1D fourier transform of the detector function (a projection) at an angle $\theta$ is the same as a line or radial slice through the origin of the 2D fourier transform of the image at the same angle. The more projections that we take, the more radial slices we obtain, and at a range of 180 degrees, (due to symmetry of a circle) we have anough radial slices to cover the entire domain and consttruct the full fourier representation of the image. At a limited range however, only the radial lines corresponding to the 40 degree wedge are populated with radial lines, and the rest of the fourier space is entirely empty. No reconstructive algorithm can recover information that is not in the data, which results in deficiencies, and regardless of how many projections we take in this range, there will be almost no improvement due to the data needed simply not being there. \\

\noindent As discussed in Lecture 11 (02/03/2026), edges appear when there is a sharp change in the brightness or intensity in the image. We characterise them using the image gradient, where the gradient vector points in the direction of maximum intensity change, and its magnitude is largest at an edge. Therefore, the direction of an edge is perpendicular to the direction of the gradient vector. Connected with the Fourier Slice Theorem, an edge will only be visible in CT ifthe projection angle perpendicular to it lies withinthe measured range. This is precisely why edges disappear at limited angle ranges, as for a 40 degree range, only edges that are perpendicular tothe projections taken there will show up inthe reconstruction, and all others will be invisible regardless of how many projections we take or which reconstruction algorithm is used. \\

\subsubsection*{(d) MLEM/OSEM vs SIRT}

\noindent Both CT and PET reconstructions can be framed as minimisation problems, however the type of noise encountered in each type of imaging is different, meaning that the algoriths used for reconstructions will also differ. \\

\noindent The forward model in CT is $p = -\log(\frac{I}{I_0}) = \sum_i \mu_i l_i$, where the dominant noise is a mix of predominantly electronic Gaussian noise and Poisson photon counting noise (which also approximates to a Gaussian at high photon counts). The maximum likelihood estimate for Gaussian noise is obtained using least squares, which is the minimisation used in Gradient Descent (SIRT) algorithms.\\

\noindent PET detects individual photon pairs from positron annihilation events. This is a discrete counting process where the acquisition model is $I_j^{em} = \sum_i I_i l_{ij}$, and $I_j^{em}$ is the number of detected photon pairs along the line of response $j$. Because PET is counting individual events, its noise follows a Poisson distribution, and the data is very noise, with additional complexity from randomness, scatter events, detector calibration and pile up factors. Due to the nature of the noise, we cannot use a least squares minimisation, but maximise using the Poisson log likelihood. The MLEM (Maximum Likelihood Expectation Maximisation) and OSEM (Ordered Subsets Expectation Maximisation) algorithms do exactly that.\\

\noindent MLEM uses a multiplicative update rather than the additive step used in SIRT, which naturally controls non-negativity and correctly accounts for the Poisson statistics of the measurements, whereas OSEM is the ordered subset version of MLEM, the PET equivalent of using OS-SART in CT. It splits data into subsets to accelerate the convergence of the MLEM algorithm, providing a good reconstruction faster than MLEM.\\

In summary, we use MLEM and OSEM in PET because the reconstruction algorithm must match the noise model of the data. While CT is mainly Gaussian and therefore suits SIRT like algorithms, PET measurements are dominated by Poisson noise, requiring a statistically correct algorithm that reflects this.

\subsubsection*{(d) Discussion and further improvements}

\noindent Gaussian FIltering produced a slightly improved reduction in noise and was consistent across all 6 coils. It works through convolution with a gaussian kernel, which averages each pixel with its neighbours weighted by their distance. Because of the nature of this filter, it is unable to distinguish between frequencies coming from noise and signal, so results in blurring both. This produced a slight bur across the image, which is particularly notiacable when comparing the edges of the origian with the gaussian filter, as they appear much softer. This is a major disadvantage for MRIimages, as sharp edges are extremely important to be able to identify the anatomical structures present in the scan.\\

\noindent Bilateral filtering is an extension of Gaussian filtering, and additionally weights neighbours by the similarity in their intensity, which prevents pixels along a strong edge being averaged together. In principle, this should smooth flat regions while preserving edges, which was the case for Coils 1, 5 and 6, but the filter struggled with the low dynamic range of the magnitude images, and performed particularly badly on Coil 3, as the signal intensity for this coil was roughly 0.02--0.12, whereas the rest of the coils had a range of 0.2--1.4, and this significant change meant that the parameters set were not optimal at a range so small. The parameters for this filter were chosen empirically and set to $d=10$, $\sigma_{\text{color}}=0.2$, $\sigma_{\text{space}}=8$, and the trial and error highlighted exactly how sensitive this filter is to the parameters provided.\\

\noindent Wavelet filtering was the most consitent across all coils. It decomposes each image into wavelet coefficients, estimates noise levels and applies soft thresholding, which is particularly suited to MRI imaging as it can distinguish between noise and genuine signal containing structural details, something that is lost in the Gaussian filter. It is also able to preserve the sharpness of all edges, and performed well on all the coils, despite coil 3 having a signal intensity an order of magnitude smaller than the others. This placed it above The Gaussian and Bilateral fitlers, as not only was it able to reduce noise without compromising edge sharpness and anatomical structures, but it did so on all the coils. When applied to the combined image, it is clear that the structure and edges are clearer and the noise is reduced, particularly in the darker areas, where the salt and pepper noise is not there anymore.\\ 

\noindent The Butterworth filter operates differently to the previous ones, as it attenuates high frequencies directly in k-space before reconstruction, rather than smoothing the image afterwards. The parameters I selected differed from those provided in the example code, as after testing values of D0=10 and 30, the images produced still had excessive blurring affecting the fine details, while a value of 150 showed no improvement from the original image. I found D0=100 to be the best compromise, which was confirmed by both the magnitude and phase plot. The background noise was reduced and plots were clear and sharp, with the phase plot confirming this with a much smoother gradient distribution than in the original image. A key advantage of this method is that it can target noise and artefacts that are specific to k-space, which cannot be done in image space as the artefacts are already recostruted into the image.\\

\noindent To improve the results I could apply the butterworth filter to all six coils prior to combining them using the root sum of squares which would reduce noise at the source level and produce a clearer combined image. Another method to implement would be combining the coils by weighting them according to their individual sensitivities rather than through equal weight root sum of squares. This would weigh down coils that contribute a lot of noise in aprticular regions, providing better quality combinations. Finally, applying deep learning methods to denoise the images could learn noise distributions from training data and produce cleaner reconstructions than with classical methods.

\subsection*{(b) Summaries and critical comparison}

\subsubsection{Paper summaries:}

\noindent The method implemented is a classical region growing approach, where a point inside the tumor is chosen by the user, and the algorithm expands outwards by checking the colour intensity values of neighbouring pixels, accepting points if they are similar within a certain threshold. While successful, the basic area growth algorithm had several disadvantages in that it was largely reliant on the point within the tumor selected by the user, and struggled when the tumor was attached to the lung wall or had a similar colour intensity to the anatomy surrounding it. The paper focused on exploring different ways of enhancing this algorithm, such as using contrast augmentation to preprocess the images, implementing local thresholding to compute the intensity acceptance threshold from the tumor region rather than the entire image, growing from multiple seed points stemmed from the user's initial choice to reduce dependence on the user selecting a good point, and adding in an edge refinement step that probes boundary regions at different angles. The enhanced algorithm was tested on 60 CT scans across the DIR, LCA, NSCLC and LIDC datasets, and performance was evaluated using the Dice coefficient, which achieved a value of $0.92 \pm 0.03$, compared to $0.80 \pm 0.02$ for the basic growth algorithm. Table 10 in the paper also illustrates the accuracy of the algorithm, $92.1\%$ for the EAG, and only $84.7\%$ for the basic algorithm.\\

\noindent The Kashyap et al., ‘Automated Deep Learning–Based Detection and Segmentation of Lung Tumors at CT Imaging’. (2025) paper develops a 3D U-Net ensemble model for detecting and segmenting lung tumors on CT scans. It was trained on 1504 pre-radiotherapy CT simulation scans and the associated clinical 3D segmentations used for radiation therapy planning from 1295 patients. The ensemble combines 2 3D U-Nets, one trained for high resolution and small input volume, and one trained for low resolution and large input volume, acheiving a 92\% sensitivity and 82\% specificity in tumor detection, and a median Dice similarity coefficient (DSC) of 0.77 for segmentation, performing near the level of expert physicians (the median DSC for physician-physician was 0.80). The model was also successful in reducing the time taken to segment tumors, taking an average of 76.6 seconds compared to over 160 seconds for manual contouring. While trained on CT scans containing only one tunor, the model was also evaluated on a separate dataset containing a mean on 4.95 tumors per scan, and acheived a sensitivity of 67\%.

\subsubsection{Critical comparison:}

\noindent The papers both aim to tackle the same clinical problem of accurate lung tumor segmentation on CT images. \\

\noindent The EAG algorithm is semi-automatic, requiring a user to select the algorithm start point and specify the maximum tumor radius prior to the algorithm running. This requires the user to have good anatomical knowledge and be able to correctly read and identify the different areas on a CT scan so that a suitable point is selected. While the Enhanced Area Growth algorithm implements the multipoint-growth-starting-point, the quality of the results obtained are still slightly dependent on the original point selected. In contrast the deep learning model is entirely automatic, taking raw CT inputs and outputting both the tumor detection and segmentation without any guidance. This is major advantage, especially for workflows where manual interaction for every scan would not be feasible. It also allows users without specific knowledge in human anatomy or CT scan segmentation to interact with the model.\\

\noindent The EAG algorithm reported a higher Dice coefficient of 0.92, compared to 0.77 in the deep learning model. However, I believe these are not adequate comparisons, as in my opinion the deep learning model provided a much more robust performance analysis here. The EAG algorithm was only evaluated on 60 images, and the ground truth used for the calculation of the coefficient was only delineated by a single expert, compared to the deep learning model's evaluation on 100 images compared to three physician segmentations per scan, comprising of one clinical and two research segmentations. It is also important to note that the EAG algorithm uses a seed point that already tells it where the tumor is, while the deep learning model has to detect it first. Furthermore, the deep learning model also provided an interphysician Dice coefficient of 0.80, which when compared to the model's value of 0.77 suggests that the model is segmenting tumors at a near expert level. This is another big strength of Kashyap et al., as we are provided with a clear benchmark that allows direct comparison between human and model performence. \\

\noindent Both methods illustrate different challenges, with the EAG algorithm being unable to segment fragmented or multiple tumors across the lungs due to its seed point initialisation, and struggling when tumors impede onto the lung wall or are located in areas of similar colour intensity. The paper discusses a lung masking step to help mitigate this issue, but it doesn't fully resolve it, as the algorithm is simply unable to distinguish the tumor when it is surrounded by tissue of similar density. The deep learning model struggled with very large tumors, particularly those invading the mediastinum or chest wall. The reason provided was that large tumors were unable to fit inside the 3D input patches of the model, which meant that it could not be detected and segmented properly, or the volume of the tumor was underestimated. The EAG algrithm performed much better on large tumors, as it was not restricted to a patch or area, and could freely interpolate between its multiple growth points. While it was only trained on data containing single tumors, the deep learning model was evaluated on scans contianing multiple tumors and metastatic lesions, and performed well in this area. \\

\noindent In terms of accesibility, the EAG model is smaller and works immediately on a single image with minimal prior information, so can be used by virtually anyone due to its minimal compute requirements and setup time. The deep learning model requires training on a large and correctly labelled dataset in order to be successful, and requires GPU infrastructure for both training and inference, which is not as readily available as the machines suited for running EAG. Despite this, once set up, the model is able to segment tumors in roughly 77 seconds compared to an avergae of 170 seconds for physicians, which is a significant improvement.\\

Im summary, the Enhanced Area Growth algorithm is much simpler to implement and requires minimal computational resources while still being able to acheive very high accuracy scores on well defined tumors when correctly set up and initialised. However, the algorithm is semi-automatic and very parameter sensitive, so will only acheive this level of performance in an ideal scenario. The algorithm has also not been validated as very rigorously, as although it reports the Dice coeffienct, acceptance rates and precision, it only uses a single expert reference, which opens questions about the reliability of that benchmark. The deep learning approach is evaluated much more thoroughly, reporting detection metrics, segmentation metrics, volume agreement analysis and timing comparisons. The evaluation also implements a negative control set , evaluating breast cancer CT scans with no lung tumors to test how specific the model. It does all of this while also benchmarking against three independent physicians, whose analysis is further evaluated against eachother in order to obtain the best human to model comparison possible. The model does however require much more data and compute to develop, but once trained it is fully automatic and approaches expert level performance, making it better suited to large scale tasks. \\

\subsubsection*{(c) Proposed improvements}

\noindent Focusing on the deep learning paper, there are several key areas for improvement to explore. The 3D patch-based approach of the paper results in difficulties detecting and segmenting large tumors as they may not fit within a single patch. Implementing a coarse to fine pipeline could aid in solving this problem, as by implementing a first pass model that operates on the entire CT volume or with very large patches, tumours can be roughly identified before refining the segmentation with a second pass model that focuses on the areas around each detected tumour. This way, the first pass would be able to capture large tumours and improve the model's overall performance in this area.\\

\noindent Another improvement worth exploring is expanding the dataset the model is trained on. At present, the model is trained on CT images where not all of the tumours are segmented. The dataset includes a mix of images containing only one tumour, or multiple tumours where not all of them are always labelled. Changing this approach to include images where all tumours are accurately labelled and segmented, as well as including more images contianing multiple tumours would help generalise the model, and improve its performance in multi tumour evaluation, which currently sits at 67\%.\\

Incorporating PET data is also an important advancement, as the worst reported cases all involved tumours that invaded into the chest wall or mediastinum, and CT alone makes these boundaries too ambiguous to distinguish. PET data highlights tumours from the surrounding tissue, and could be incorporated into the model as an additional input dimension, which would not only make the model perform better, but also more suited to a variety of lung tumours, straying away from the need for perfect data and towards success in real clinical settings. This would require more training on a mix of CT and PET datasets, but would overall be a big improvement.
